---

## Section Structure

### 1. Defining the Problem

Open with a clear statement of the topic under discussion. Establish:

- **What** is being discussed
- **When** — temporal context (current trend, recent event, future projection)
- **Where** — scope (global, industry-specific, regional)
- **Who** — demographics affected or interested

Keep this section to 1–2 paragraphs. Be direct.

### 2. Related Topics & Impact

Connect the core topic to adjacent ideas the reader likely cares about. Examples:

- How this relates to their daily work or decisions
- What adjacent fields or industries are affected
- Why this matters now rather than five years ago

This section builds the reader's investment in the rest of the article.

### 3. Proposed Solution / Direction

Present the working thesis, proposed approach, or emerging consensus. Include:

- The core claim or recommendation
- Conditions under which it holds
- Supporting evidence or reasoning
- Practical implications

This is the longest section. Use subheadings if needed.

### 4. Remaining Uncertainties

Honestly address:

- Fragile assumptions in the proposed solution
- Scenarios where the solution fails
- Open questions that need further investigation
- Risks of following this direction

This section builds credibility. Do not skip it.

### 5. Further Reading (Optional)

List 3–5 accessible references with one-line descriptions:

- **[Title](URL)** — Brief description of what the reader will find
- **[Title](URL)** — Brief description

Omit this section entirely if:

- The discussion is purely speculative or fictional
- No quality references exist
- The topic is too niche for accessible reading

## Tone Guidelines

- Academic structure, conversational voice
- First-person perspective when documenting personal observations
- Third-person for general analysis
- Avoid jargon without explanation
- Target reading level: educated general audience
- Length: 1200–1500 words (~5 minute read)
